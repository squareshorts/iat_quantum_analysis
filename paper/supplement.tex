\section*{Supplementary Methods}
% =================================================

\subsection*{Binning and normalization}

Within each participant and critical block, trials were ordered by presentation sequence
and mapped to a normalized position variable $x \in [0,1]$. Reaction times were
aggregated into quantile bins with $N_{\text{bins}} \in \{4,6,8\}$. Participants with
fewer than three populated bins were excluded so that the cosine--sine basis remained
identifiable.

For predictive validation, standardization was performed in a leak-free manner:
z-scores were computed from training bins only and then applied to held-out bins.
For the estimation of the pooled interference angle $\theta$, all bins were used for
standardization to maintain consistency with the Gaussian observation model used in
profiling.

\subsection*{Numerical stability}

Closed-form ordinary least squares (OLS) estimation was used throughout. To stabilize
matrix inversion in near-degenerate cases, a ridge penalty of $10^{-5}$ was added to
$X^\top X$. Results were insensitive to the precise value of this penalty within
several orders of magnitude.

\subsection*{Robustness of the pooled posterior to binning resolution}

To assess sensitivity to temporal discretization, the full profiling pipeline was
repeated using 4, 6, and 8 quantile bins per participant. The inferred pooled posterior
remained concentrated in the low-angle regime across all three resolutions, although the
posterior mode shifted modestly with the number of bins---from $22.75^\circ$ (4 bins)
to $17.25^\circ$ (6 bins) to $15.50^\circ$ (8 bins). All three posteriors are sharply
concentrated (SD $<1^\circ$). This pattern supports robustness of the low-angle
solution while also showing that it is not numerically invariant to discretization.

\subsection*{Population-level bootstrap stability}

To assess whether the pooled $\theta$ estimate is stable across random participant draws,
a bootstrap subsampling analysis was performed. One hundred independent subsamples of
$N=30{,}000$ participants each were drawn from the full dataset (with replacement); for
each subsample the complete grid-profiling procedure was repeated. The posterior MAP
equalled $17^\circ$ on every replicate, yielding a bootstrap SD of $0.00^\circ$ and a
95\% percentile interval of $[17^\circ, 17^\circ]$. This stability shows that
the $\approx 17^\circ$--$18^\circ$ result is not a feature of any particular participant
composition. However, bootstrap stability alone does not address recovery bias: the
recovery analyses below show that at sample sizes far below the empirical corpus,
pooled inference of low angles remains biased and wide. Bootstrap evidence is therefore
interpreted together with the $N$-scaling and simulation-based recovery results, not as
a standalone validation.

\centering

\caption{Bootstrap stability of the pooled \(\theta\) estimate. Each of 100 replicates
resamples 30{,}000 participants and recomputes the pooled MAP. The bootstrap
distribution is effectively degenerate around the empirical solution, indicating
extremely high stability of the population-level estimate at large $N$.}
\label{fig:supp-bootstrap}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Expanded simulation-based recovery}

Recovery failure in the low-angle regime is the central limitation of the pooled angular
inference framework. Simulation-based recovery was conducted using 100 independent
replicates for each of 11 generating angles spanning $5^\circ$--$120^\circ$, with
$N=5{,}000$ simulated participant curves per replicate. Recovery was performed on the
full $0^\circ$--$180^\circ$ profiling grid (0.25-degree steps).

Recovery is near-unbiased and yields coverage $\geq 91\%$ for
$\theta_{\text{true}} \geq 40^\circ$. For small angles ($\theta_{\text{true}} \leq
25^\circ$), recovery is substantially biased toward the range $38^\circ$--$44^\circ$,
and 94\% HDI coverage drops to 0\%--50\%. This asymmetry is geometrically expected:
for $\theta \approx 17^\circ$, the cosinusoidal argument $\theta \cdot x$ spans only
a narrow range over the trial sequence, producing a nearly flat temporal modulation
difficult to distinguish from additive trends at $N=5{,}000$.

The poor small-angle recovery under $N=5{,}000$ does not invalidate the empirical
population-level estimate, but it does constrain interpretation. At the empirically
observed scale ($N=141{,}329$), the profile posterior integrates signal across a far
larger number of observations. The bootstrap analysis above---in which $30{,}000$-participant
subsamples consistently recover $17^\circ$---combined with the $N$-scaling analysis
below shows that population-level identification enters a credible regime around and
above the empirical dataset size. Credibility here is conditional on large-scale pooling:
low-angle inference cannot be claimed at small or moderate sample sizes.

\subsection*{Recovery as a function of sample size}

To clarify why the empirical low-angle estimate is stable despite poor recovery at small
sample size, recovery was examined as a function of $N$. Small and moderate samples
($N \lesssim 10{,}000$) remain biased upward with 0\%--50\% coverage, whereas the
empirical dataset falls in a regime where the recovered MAP aligns with the observed
estimate and the HDI covers the target angle.

\subsection*{Objective comparison: profiling posterior versus hold-out predictive criterion}

Posterior density, normalized RSS, hold-out RMSE, and log-predictive-score were
compared on a common $\theta$ grid for each of the three binning conditions. The
hold-out RMSE is minimized at the boundary $\theta = 0^\circ$ for all three bin counts,
whereas the profile posterior peaks in the low-angle interior. This divergence reflects
a mismatch between objective functions, not a contradiction in the results. Hold-out
RMSE is a local predictive criterion that rewards short-horizon interpolation of the
last bins after participant-wise refitting. The profiled interference angle targets a
global structural parameter---the pooled geometric relation that organizes contextual
overlap across curves. These two criteria are not aligned, and the figure makes the
distinction explicit.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{ext_objectives_vs_theta.png}
\caption{Population-level objectives evaluated on the same $\theta$ grid for 4, 6, and
8 bins. The posterior density peaks in the low-angle interior, whereas the hold-out
RMSE optimum occurs at $\theta=0^\circ$ in all three binning resolutions.}
\label{fig:supp-objectives}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Additional model comparison details}

Predictive performance was evaluated using participant-wise temporal hold-out, in which
the highest-$x$ $\approx 20\%$ of bins were reserved for testing. Root mean squared
error (RMSE) was computed on held-out bins after leak-free standardization. At 6 bins,
differences in RMSE between all models are small in absolute magnitude: the interference
model (RMSE $= 21.919$) and the polynomial baseline (RMSE $= 21.924$) are
effectively tied, while cosine-only, exponential, and power-law boundary cases
achieve RMSE values near $21.896$--$21.899$. The full interference model does not
achieve the lowest hold-out error, consistent with the objective-mismatch pattern
described above.

\subsection*{Null-geometry controls}

\subsubsection*{Within-curve permutation null (500 replicates)}

To test whether the recovered contextual overlap angle reflects genuine temporal
structure rather than smoothness or binning artefacts, bin order was randomly permuted
within participant curves and the profiling procedure was repeated. Five hundred
permutation replicates were generated. Under permutation, null posterior MAPs scattered
broadly (mean $= 109.65^\circ$, SD $= 67.79^\circ$, covering the full
$[0^\circ, 180^\circ]$ interval), with mean posterior SD averaging $7.72^\circ$---16
times wider than the observed SD of $0.48^\circ$.

However, this null is weak and non-decisive. Concentrated, boundary-seeking posteriors
still appear in some permutation replicates, so posterior sharpness alone does not rule
out alternative sources of angular concentration. The permutation comparison provides
one constraint on interpretation but should not be treated as strong confirming evidence
for the geometric model.

\subsubsection*{Quadratic null (300 replicates)}

Synthetic data were generated from the empirical polynomial (quadratic) coefficient
distribution and subjected to the same profiling procedure. Three hundred replicates
($N=5{,}000$ per replicate) were assessed on the full $0^\circ$--$180^\circ$ grid.
The quadratic null produced MAP estimates centred near $42^\circ$ (mean $= 42.25^\circ$,
SD $= 13.59^\circ$, mean HDI width $41.70^\circ$), substantially broader and
higher in entropy (mean $= 5.20$ nats) than the observed posterior (HDI width
$1.75^\circ$, entropy $2.08$ nats). Additive polynomial trends do not produce the
sharp low-angle concentration present in the real data.

\subsection*{Residual structure and held-out distributional fit}

Near-tied RMSE on participant-level temporal hold-out does not imply equivalent model
structure. On block-curve hold-out data, the interference and quadratic model RMSEs
are nearly indistinguishable ($57.218$ vs.\ $57.235$), but their residual maps differ
systematically. The quadratic model leaves stronger negative drift in the last held-out
bin, particularly in the higher-angle blocks 4 and 7 (mean absolute residual in the
final held-out bin: $0.476$ vs.\ $0.381$ for the interference fit). The magnitude of
quadratic residuals also correlates more strongly with the blockwise pooled angle
($r = 0.240$) than the interference residuals do ($r = 0.130$).

Considering the full held-out distribution, rather than pointwise RMSE, further
discriminates the models. Across the eight held-out block/bin cells, the interference
fit yields lower Wasserstein distance, lower quantile error, and lower interquartile-range
error in every cell (cell means: $1.022$ vs.\ $1.323$, $0.956$ vs.\ $1.283$, and
$1.524$ vs.\ $2.070$, respectively). The additive model can match local pointwise error
while still misrepresenting the shape of the held-out response distribution.

\subsection*{External leverage: D-like quintiles and independent life-satisfaction IAT}

\subsubsection*{D-like congruency quintiles}

To test whether the recovered angle is reducible to a conventional IAT effect-size
summary, the Gender--Science corpus was stratified into quintiles of a D-like
congruency score (difference between incongruent and congruent mean latencies divided
by participant SD). Mean D-like values vary substantially across quintiles (from
$-0.684$ in the lowest to $0.788$ in the highest), yet the subgroup posteriors remain
tightly clustered in the same low-angle regime. Quintile MAP estimates range only from
$17^\circ$ to $18^\circ$, with HDIs spanning roughly $15^\circ$--$20^\circ$. The
angular summary is not captured by the conventional congruency metric.

\subsubsection*{Independent life-satisfaction IAT}

An independent 2024 life-satisfaction IAT (Testable protocol, five phases) was
analyzed as an out-of-sample contrast. The raw archive contained 187 per-participant
CSV files; after excluding repeated names (retaining first attempt) and participants
with $>10\%$ of raw attempts faster than 300 ms, and collapsing correction loops,
180 valid participants were retained. Profiling was restricted to the critical combined
phases (phases 3 and 5).

The resulting posterior concentrates at the near-orthogonal boundary: posterior mean
$177.48^\circ$, SD $2.68^\circ$, MAP $180.0^\circ$, 94\% HDI $[172.75^\circ,
180.0^\circ]$. This stands in sharp contrast to the low-angle Gender--Science solution
and is too large a shift to be explained by numerical regularization alone. The same
estimation procedure that recovers partial contextual overlap in the Gender--Science
IAT recovers near-orthogonal geometry in the independent task, confirming that the
angle tracks task context.

On the independent task, the full interference model outperforms the quadratic baseline
in leak-free hold-out RMSE ($5.230$ vs.\ $5.607$), while the best-performing model is
the cosine-only boundary case (RMSE $4.804$). This shows that angular geometry carries
predictive leverage when the task occupies a different contextual regime, and that the
full sine-plus-cosine form is not always necessary near the orthogonal boundary.

\subsection*{Block-dependent phase structure}

Block-specific pooled phase parameters were estimated to examine whether the inferred
interference angle reflects a stationary property or an aggregate over heterogeneous
contexts. The resulting structure exhibits systematic variation. Block 3 is associated
with a lower pooled angle (MAP $= 21.00^\circ$), whereas blocks 4, 6, and 7 cluster at
higher values (MAPs ranging from $26.25^\circ$ to $29.00^\circ$). This indicates that
contextual overlap is not constant across the IAT and that the aggregate interference
angle recovered at the population level represents a mixture over block-specific
geometries rather than a single stationary parameter.

Blockwise quadratic coefficients, by contrast, point in nearly the same direction in
the $(d_1,d_2)$ plane (coefficient angles $145^\circ$--$148^\circ$) and differ mainly
in norm. The polynomial model can absorb block-dependent variation only by refitting
nuisance coefficients within each block; those coefficients do not map onto a single
interpretable geometric dimension. The angle $\theta$ places different blocks on a
common contextual-rotation scale---low-dimensional structure that local RMSE does not
reward.

% =================================================
\section*{Limits of individual-level interpretation}
% =================================================

Participant-specific interference angles can be estimated, but they are weakly identified
under sparse binning. The recovery analyses show that low true angles are
especially vulnerable to upward bias and poor interval coverage when fewer than roughly
$10{,}000$ participants are aggregated. In the present design, $\theta$ is therefore
best interpreted as a pooled descriptor of contextual overlap at the population level
rather than as a stable individual-difference parameter. Population-level identification
is conditional on large-scale pooling: the bootstrap analysis shows that
$\theta \approx 17^\circ$--$18^\circ$ is consistently recovered from any large random
subsample ($N \geq 30{,}000$) of the dataset, but the $N$-scaling analyses confirm that
this stability does not generalize to small or moderate sample sizes.

\section*{Reproducibility}
% =================================================

All analyses were performed using deterministic grid profiling and closed-form regression
rather than stochastic posterior sampling. Scripts and configuration files are provided
with the project materials so that all reported results can be regenerated.
